I. Historical Background and the Rise of Chancery

The English system of Equity arose as a response to the rigidity of the common law. In medieval England, the King’s courts operated on strict forms of action and writs. When those forms proved inadequate, aggrieved parties petitioned the King for relief “in conscience.” These petitions were referred to the Lord Chancellor—often a cleric— regarded as the “keeper of the King’s conscience.” Over time, the Court of Chancery formalized equitable proceedings. Its purpose was not to contradict the common law, but to supplement it where legal remedies were incomplete or unjust.

John Norton Pomeroy explains that Equity “supplements the law by affording relief in cases of rights recognized by the law, but for which its remedies are inadequate.” See A-Treatise-On-Equity-Jurisprudence-1881-Vol1 (Pomeroy) . Equity developed its own bills, injunctions, and decrees—distinct from writs and judgments—yet its aim remained justice rather than technicality.

II. The Nature and Purpose of Equity

Equity is more than moral sentiment; it is a juridical system guided by conscience, reason, and justice. Pomeroy emphasizes that Equity does not create rights unknown to the law; rather, it recognizes rights and supplies relief when forms of action at law fail. Likewise, Snell’s Principles of Equity (1885) observes Equity’s function is “to mitigate the rigour of the common law where its remedies fall short of substantial justice.”

Typical equitable domains include specific performance (when damages are inadequate), injunctions (to restrain threatened/continuing wrongs), trusts, rescission, rectification, and accounting. Equity asks: “What is just between these parties in these circumstances?”

III. The Maxims of Equity

The maxims of Equity are condensed expressions of centuries of judicial reasoning—guiding principles rather than inflexible rules. Across the classic sources, frequently cited maxims include:

  • Equity will not suffer a wrong without a remedy.
  • Equity regards as done that which ought to be done.
  • He who seeks Equity must do Equity.
  • He who comes into Equity must come with clean hands.
  • Equity aids the vigilant, not those who slumber on their rights.
  • Equality is Equity.

See the collection and commentary in: A-Collection-Of-Legal-Maxims-In-Law-And-Equity-1880 and Equity-Maxims-By-BAR-President-Tuley-1903 .

IV. The Fusion of Law and Equity

The Judicature Acts (1870s) merged the administration of law and equity, but the fusion was principally procedural. The distinct principles of Equity persisted, shaping remedies and standards within unified courts. As the Yale Law Journal (1911) observed, “to fuse procedure is not to abolish conscience.”

For a classic analysis of the risks and misunderstandings around fusion, see: CONFUSION-OF-LAW-AND-EQUITY (Yale L.J., 1911) .

V. The Continuing Relevance of Equity

Equity’s doctrines underpin modern trust law, fiduciary obligations, unjust enrichment, and equitable distribution. Remedies such as injunctions, rescission, rectification, and constructive trusts remain central. Equitable principles continue to “moralize” the law, requiring good faith and conscience in the exercise of rights and the granting of relief.

Summary and Reflection

  • Equity originated to correct the rigidity of law.
  • Its maxims remain the moral compass of judicial discretion.
  • Fusion unified courts, not conscience or principles.
  • Equitable thinking continues to shape modern remedies and duties.
